01 — Problem

A beloved cinema, a frustrating experience.

Cines Callao is one of Madrid's most iconic cultural landmarks. Yet its digital experience hadn't kept pace. Users were abandoning the booking flow mid-funnel, discovering showtimes through Google instead of the app, and avoiding digital tickets out of distrust.

The brief was clear: simplify ticket booking, improve schedule visibility, and build a mobile-first experience that actually felt like it belonged to a premium cinema — not a generic template.

02 — Research & Insights

12 conversations. 4 patterns that shaped everything.

Semi-structured interviews with regular cinema-goers (18–45) in Madrid. I wasn't just mapping usability failures — I was listening for the emotional arc of deciding to go to the cinema and the friction that killed that excitement before checkout.

🧭
Navigation paralysis
Finding showtimes took more than 3 taps. Most users opened Google instead — the app had lost the first moment of intent.
🎬
No discovery mode
"I don't always know what I want to watch." The app assumed decision-made users. Most weren't.
🎟️
Checkout as chore
Seat selection and payment felt transactional, not anticipatory. The excitement of going to the cinema was absent.
📲
Digital ticket distrust
Users printed paper backups "just in case." Zero confidence in the digital ticket meant zero adoption.
03 — Process

From sticky notes to flows that breathe.

1

Define

Jobs to be done

Synthesised research into 3 primary JTBDs: browse movies, find a showtime fast, book with confidence. Cut every feature that didn't map to a real job.

2

Explore

8 information architectures

Low-fidelity IA sketches tested with 5 users. Discarded movie-first nav in favour of dual entry — browse by film OR by showtime.

3

Build

Component system first

Built an atomic design system in Figma before any screens. Tokens for spacing, type, and colour ensured any change cascaded across 40+ artboards.

4

Test

3 rounds of usability testing

Each round had one focused hypothesis. Iterated on seat map interaction twice before passing the zero-confusion bar.

01 — Problem

Abruzzo has a lot to offer. None of it is easy to find.

Abruzzo is one of Italy's least digitally represented regions. Events, activities, and local experiences are scattered across municipal websites, Facebook groups, WhatsApp chats, and niche organiser pages. For tourists, that means missed opportunities. For locals, it's the same.

The goal was a single platform connecting tourists, residents, and local businesses — centralising discovery without flattening the character of the region into a generic travel feed.

02 — Research

The gap wasn't in Abruzzo. It was in the infrastructure around it.

Market research confirmed what personal experience already suggested: compared to Tuscany or the Amalfi Coast, Abruzzo has almost no dedicated digital layer for discovery. The two closest competitors — Airbnb Experiences and the local app Abruzzocosafare — both exist but neither covers the full picture.

🗂️
Scattered information
Finding events meant checking multiple websites, social media pages, and organiser platforms separately. No single source of truth existed for what's actually happening.
📅
Missed opportunities
Without a centralised source, both locals and tourists regularly miss events they would have attended. The problem isn't interest — it's visibility.
🏪
Invisible local businesses
Small operators and event organisers had no scalable way to reach an audience. Word of mouth and Facebook posts were the primary distribution channels.
🌍
Three distinct user needs
Tourists need curation and guidance. Residents want to discover what's nearby. Businesses need a channel. One app, three different jobs to support.
03 — Design decisions

One app. Three audiences. One visual identity rooted in the region.

1

Identity

Colours from the Abruzzo flag

The visual identity was grounded directly in Abruzzo's regional flag — not a generic travel palette. Green and white with red accents. The design immediately communicates where it belongs.

2

Structure

Centralised discovery

Events, activities, and local businesses in one feed. Filterable by category, date, and location — so both a tourist planning a weekend and a local checking what's on tonight find what they need.

3

Audience

Wireframes across three user journeys

Low-fidelity wireframes were built separately for each of the three user groups before moving to hi-fi, to validate that the same architecture served different entry points and goals.

4

Growth

Built for iteration

Next steps scoped out: social features for sharing events, in-app ticketing with organiser partnerships, and analytics tools for local businesses to track engagement.

01 — Problem

Same template. Different company. No one could tell.

Dicarlobus is a regional bus operator in Abruzzo serving both local commuters and tourists. Their existing app was built on a generic public transport template shared with other operators — same layout, same components, barely differentiated branding.

The significant challenge: extracting a distinct brand identity from an application that shared a generic template with other public transportation services. Every design decision had to earn its place — not just look different, but mean something to the people who relied on this service daily.

02 — Research & Insights

Commuters and tourists. One app. Completely different urgency levels.

8 user interviews across two distinct groups: daily commuters who needed speed, and tourists who needed guidance and confidence. The existing app served neither — it was built for an abstract "transit user" who didn't exist.

⏱️
Commuters need speed
"I check it on the platform with 2 minutes to spare." Daily users needed their route in one tap — not buried under content meant for first-timers.
🧳
Tourists need reassurance
Visitors couldn't tell if the schedule was live or outdated. Trust signals — timestamps, real-time indicators — were completely absent.
🎨
Generic means invisible
Multiple users thought they'd used the same app for different bus companies. Zero brand recall eroded trust before the service was even evaluated.
♿
Accessibility failures
The existing interface failed WCAG AA on contrast, touch target size, and screen reader labelling — alienating a significant user base.
03 — Process

Identity first. Interface second.

1

Brand

Extracting identity from nothing

Built a colour system, type hierarchy, and iconography language from scratch — rooted in the amber and terracotta palette of Abruzzo's landscape. Starting from the one asset Dicarlobus owned: the name.

2

Structure

Context-aware homepage

Commuters see saved routes immediately. Tourists see curated routes and onboarding. Same architecture, different surface — adapts to returning vs. first-time users.

3

Clarity

Schedules as the product

Redesigned the schedule view as the primary product surface. Realtime status, departure countdown, and platform info scannable in under 2 seconds.

4

Accessibility

WCAG AA from the token level

Rebuilt colour system with contrast ratios baked into design tokens. Every touch target audited against 44×44px minimum. Screen reader labels reviewed on every element.

01 — Problem

Self-checkout promises speed. Usually delivers frustration.

Supermarket self-checkout machines are everywhere — and almost universally disliked. Scanner errors, frozen screens, confusing interfaces, and payment dead-ends push customers back to staffed tills or out the door annoyed. The trust in automated checkout is eroding, and the interfaces aren't helping.

QuickCheckout is a kiosk UI redesign aimed at making self-checkout actually work: faster scanning, clearer error recovery, honest feedback, and a checkout flow that doesn't require a staff member to complete.

02 — Research

Tested in real supermarkets. Observed in two countries.

Research combined web observation of self-checkout coverage in Italian and American markets, a survey on checkout experiences and payment preferences, and direct personal testing across multiple supermarkets. The problems that kept surfacing were consistent regardless of brand or country.

⚙️
Frequent technical failures
Scanner malfunctions, frozen screens, and software errors were reported across every context tested. Each one breaks the promise of a faster checkout.
🧩
Interfaces designed for no one
Navigation logic that confused first-time users and offered nothing faster for experienced ones. The interface assumed a middle-ground user that doesn't exist.
💳
Payment step as a dead end
Limited or unclear payment options disrupted the final step. Survey data showed cards are the dominant preference — but the UI rarely reflected that priority.
🔄
No way back from errors
When something went wrong, the machine offered no path forward without staff assistance. The system failed without a recovery mechanism.
03 — Design decisions

Every screen answers one question: what does the user need right now?

1

Clarity

Item illustrations + photo confirmation

Each scanned item displays its photo and name. Visual confirmation reduces mis-scans and gives users real-time confidence in what's in their cart.

2

Recovery

Errors with a next step

Every error state tells the user exactly what to do next. No dead ends, no waiting — the system guides recovery without requiring staff involvement.

3

Payment

Card-first, options visible

Payment method hierarchy reflects actual usage: card primary, then meal vouchers, then cash. Loyalty cards and coupons surface at the right moment — before payment, not after.

4

Rewards

Loyalty surfacing at checkout

Loyalty rewards, coupons, and tax lottery options appear contextually during the flow — not buried in a settings menu. Reduces missed benefits and improves overall satisfaction.
