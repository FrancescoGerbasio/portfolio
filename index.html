Cines Callao

01 — Problem

Finding a movie, watching the trailer, and buying a ticket shouldn't be three separate tasks.

Moviegoers in Madrid bounce between YouTube for trailers, cinema websites for showtimes, and separate booking platforms to complete a purchase. Each step loses people. The experience is fragmented by design — not by necessity.

Cines Callao is a concept app for one of Madrid's most iconic cinemas. The brief was to collapse the full journey — discover, watch, decide, book — into a single, trailer-first experience. Not affiliated with Cines Callao; designed as an independent concept.

02 — Research

5 interviews. A competitive audit. Two rounds of testing.

Research started with a competitive audit of Spanish cinema chains and indirect competitors — streaming and aggregator platforms — to map how trailer discovery, seat selection, and booking were handled across the market. Then 5 in-person interviews with Madrid-based moviegoers at different life stages, conducted at the hostel where I was staying at the time.

📱
Trailers live outside the booking flow
Users go to YouTube or social media to watch trailers, then have to start over on a different platform to book. The decision moment and the purchase moment are disconnected.
⚡
Busy users want zero friction
Professionals and casual watchers described wanting to open an app, pick a film, and book in under two minutes. Any extra step was a reason to drop off.
👨‍👩‍👧
Families need age info upfront
Parents checking if a film is suitable for children had to dig — ratings were buried or absent. Age appropriateness and seat availability needed to be visible before the booking flow, not inside it.
🎬
Film enthusiasts want depth and shareability
Engaged moviegoers wanted cast bios, director background, and a way to share trailers directly. The social layer around cinema was missing entirely from existing apps.
03 — Design & Testing

Trailer first. Booking one tap away. Tested and iterated twice.

1

Structure

Trailer-first homepage

Homepage leads with a video trailer montage of current releases, backed by a movie poster carousel. The decision to watch comes before the decision to book — matching how people actually discover films.

2

Flow

One-tap booking from the detail screen

Film detail screen combines trailer, cast, ratings, showtimes, and seat selection without leaving the screen. Booking is one continuous scroll — not a separate flow users have to navigate to.

3

Testing R1

5 in-person participants → maturity badges added

Round 1 feedback: users liked suggestions but needed age ratings visible on the browse level. Introduced maturity rating badges for quick scanning before entering a film's detail page.

4

Testing R2

Remote test → confirmation screen redesigned

Round 2 feedback: the booking confirmation felt thin. Redesigned to include full ticket details, QR code, and a clear summary — giving users the confidence that their booking was complete.

Homepage → Movie detail → Booking confirmation

Booking time cut by 30%. Every friction point identified, addressed, and retested.

01 — Problem

Self-checkout promises speed. Usually delivers frustration.

Supermarket self-checkout machines are everywhere — and almost universally disliked. Scanner errors, frozen screens, confusing interfaces, and payment dead-ends push customers back to staffed tills or out the door annoyed. The trust in automated checkout is eroding, and the interfaces aren't helping.

QuickCheckout is a kiosk UI redesign aimed at making self-checkout actually work: faster scanning, clearer error recovery, honest feedback, and a checkout flow that doesn't require a staff member to complete.

02 — Research

Tested in real supermarkets. Observed in two countries.

Research combined web observation of self-checkout coverage in Italian and American markets, a survey on checkout experiences and payment preferences, and direct personal testing across multiple supermarkets. The problems that kept surfacing were consistent regardless of brand or country.

⚙️
Frequent technical failures
Scanner malfunctions, frozen screens, and software errors were reported across every context tested. Each one breaks the promise of a faster checkout.
🧩
Interfaces designed for no one
Navigation logic that confused first-time users and offered nothing faster for experienced ones. The interface assumed a middle-ground user that doesn't exist.
💳
Payment step as a dead end
Limited or unclear payment options disrupted the final step. Survey data showed cards are the dominant preference — but the UI rarely reflected that priority.
🔄
No way back from errors
When something went wrong, the machine offered no path forward without staff assistance. The system failed without a recovery mechanism.
03 — Design decisions

Every screen answers one question: what does the user need right now?

1

Clarity

Item illustrations + photo confirmation

Each scanned item displays its photo and name. Visual confirmation reduces mis-scans and gives users real-time confidence in what's in their cart.

2

Recovery

Errors with a next step

Every error state tells the user exactly what to do next. No dead ends, no waiting — the system guides recovery without requiring staff involvement.

3

Payment

Card-first, options visible

Payment method hierarchy reflects actual usage: card primary, then meal vouchers, then cash. Loyalty cards and coupons surface at the right moment — before payment, not after.

4

Rewards

Loyalty surfacing at checkout

Loyalty rewards, coupons, and tax lottery options appear contextually during the flow — not buried in a settings menu. Reduces missed benefits and improves overall satisfaction.

Scan → Error recovery → Payment

Self-checkout that actually recovers — without calling for help.

01 — Problem

Abruzzo has a lot to offer. None of it is easy to find.

Abruzzo is one of Italy's least digitally represented regions. Events, activities, and local experiences are scattered across municipal websites, Facebook groups, WhatsApp chats, and niche organiser pages. For tourists, that means missed opportunities. For locals, it's the same.

The goal was a single platform connecting tourists, residents, and local businesses — centralising discovery without flattening the character of the region into a generic travel feed.

02 — Research

The gap wasn't in Abruzzo. It was in the infrastructure around it.

Market research confirmed what personal experience already suggested: compared to Tuscany or the Amalfi Coast, Abruzzo has almost no dedicated digital layer for discovery. The two closest competitors — Airbnb Experiences and the local app Abruzzocosafare — both exist but neither covers the full picture.

🗂️
Scattered information
Finding events meant checking multiple websites, social media pages, and organiser platforms separately. No single source of truth existed for what's actually happening.
📅
Missed opportunities
Without a centralised source, both locals and tourists regularly miss events they would have attended. The problem isn't interest — it's visibility.
🏪
Invisible local businesses
Small operators and event organisers had no scalable way to reach an audience. Word of mouth and Facebook posts were the primary distribution channels.
🌍
Three distinct user needs
Tourists need curation and guidance. Residents want to discover what's nearby. Businesses need a channel. One app, three different jobs to support.
03 — Design decisions

One app. Three audiences. One visual identity rooted in the region.

1

Identity

Colours from the Abruzzo flag

The visual identity was grounded directly in Abruzzo's regional flag — not a generic travel palette. Green and white with red accents. The design immediately communicates where it belongs.

2

Structure

Centralised discovery

Events, activities, and local businesses in one feed. Filterable by category, date, and location — so both a tourist planning a weekend and a local checking what's on tonight find what they need.

3

Audience

Wireframes across three user journeys

Low-fidelity wireframes were built separately for each of the three user groups before moving to hi-fi, to validate that the same architecture served different entry points and goals.

4

Growth

Built for iteration

Next steps scoped out: social features for sharing events, in-app ticketing with organiser partnerships, and analytics tools for local businesses to track engagement.

Discover → Event detail → Explore nearby

01 — Problem

Same template. Different company. No one could tell.

Dicarlobus is a regional bus operator in Abruzzo. Their existing app was built on a generic public transport template shared with other operators — same layout, same structure, barely differentiated branding. The result was an interface with no identity, cluttered with information users didn't need, and not properly optimised for mobile.

The redesign had to solve three things at once: extract a distinct brand from a generic starting point, reduce visual noise, and make the core job — finding and booking a bus — fast on a phone.

02 — Research

Competitive analysis + personal experience with low-cost bus apps.

Research combined competitive analysis against two reference points — Flixbus as the UX benchmark, Clickbus as a negative comparison with similar problems to Dicarlobus — and direct personal experience using multiple bus travel apps, including gathering feedback from other users during real journeys.

🎨
No brand, no trust
Sharing a generic layout with other operators meant Dicarlobus was indistinguishable. Users had no visual cue that they were dealing with a specific, accountable company.
🗂️
Cluttered interface
Information overload made it hard to find essential details quickly — a critical failure for an app people open with one hand at a bus stop.
📱
Not built for mobile
The interface hadn't been properly adapted for small screens. Key actions were buried, touch targets were tight, and the layout broke the context of how people actually use transit apps.
🚌
Flixbus as the benchmark
Flixbus showed what a well-executed bus app looks like — clear routes, fast booking, strong brand. It set the standard the redesign had to meet for the regional market.
03 — Design decisions

Brand first. Then simplify everything else.

1

Brand

Colours pulled directly from the buses

The Dicarlobus buses are orange and blue. That became the design system: orange for tickets and active states, blue for the bus pass. Not invented — extracted from what already existed in the real world.

2

Process

Medium-fidelity wireframes first

Started with a generic mid-fi version focused purely on fixing the structural problems — information hierarchy, navigation clarity, mobile layout — before applying any visual identity.

3

Simplification

Reduced information density

Prioritised what a user needs to complete their journey: route, time, ticket, QR code. Everything else was secondary or removed entirely from the primary flow.

4

Mobile

Rebuilt for one-handed use

Primary actions repositioned within thumb reach. Ticket and booking confirmation designed to be readable at a glance — because that's the context in which this app is actually used.

Wireframe → Branded UI → Ticket confirmation

A generic template became a recognisable brand. Built from what was already there.
