01 — Problem

A beloved cinema, a frustrating experience.

Cines Callao is one of Madrid's most iconic cultural landmarks. Yet its digital experience hadn't kept pace. Users were abandoning the booking flow mid-funnel, discovering showtimes through Google instead of the app, and avoiding digital tickets out of distrust.

The brief was clear: simplify ticket booking, improve schedule visibility, and build a mobile-first experience that actually felt like it belonged to a premium cinema — not a generic template.

02 — Research & Insights

12 conversations. 4 patterns that shaped everything.

Semi-structured interviews with regular cinema-goers (18–45) in Madrid. I wasn't just mapping usability failures — I was listening for the emotional arc of deciding to go to the cinema and the friction that killed that excitement before checkout.

🧭
Navigation paralysis
Finding showtimes took more than 3 taps. Most users opened Google instead — the app had lost the first moment of intent.
🎬
No discovery mode
"I don't always know what I want to watch." The app assumed decision-made users. Most weren't.
🎟️
Checkout as chore
Seat selection and payment felt transactional, not anticipatory. The excitement of going to the cinema was absent.
📲
Digital ticket distrust
Users printed paper backups "just in case." Zero confidence in the digital ticket meant zero adoption.
03 — Process

From sticky notes to flows that breathe.

1

Define

Jobs to be done

Synthesised research into 3 primary JTBDs: browse movies, find a showtime fast, book with confidence. Cut every feature that didn't map to a real job.

2

Explore

8 information architectures

Low-fidelity IA sketches tested with 5 users. Discarded movie-first nav in favour of dual entry — browse by film OR by showtime.

3

Build

Component system first

Built an atomic design system in Figma before any screens. Tokens for spacing, type, and colour ensured any change cascaded across 40+ artboards.

4

Test

3 rounds of usability testing

Each round had one focused hypothesis. Iterated on seat map interaction twice before passing the zero-confusion bar.

Browse → Showtimes → Digital ticket

Navigation time to booking cut by 30%. Validated across three testing rounds.

01 — Problem

The checkout page kills more sales than bad products.

67% of online shopping carts are abandoned at checkout. Not because users changed their mind — because the process felt like friction, not a transaction. Too many steps, too many fields, too many reasons to pause and reconsider.

QuickCheckout was designed to compress that friction into a single, confident moment: you've already decided to buy. The UI shouldn't make you doubt that.

02 — Research & Insights

Abandonment isn't indecision. It's friction.

Competitor analysis across 8 major checkout flows, combined with 10 user interviews. The moments where users dropped off were almost always the same regardless of platform.

📋
Form fatigue
Seeing 12+ fields before payment caused users to abandon immediately. Length perception mattered more than actual effort required.
🔐
Forced account creation
"I just want to buy one thing." Guest checkout removal was the single biggest drop-off trigger identified in testing.
💳
Payment anxiety
Users needed visible trust signals at the exact moment they typed card details — not on the homepage, not in the footer.
↩️
No recovery path
Validation errors wiped filled fields, restarting the cognitive load. Users gave up instead of correcting.
03 — Process

Every field earned its place.

1

Audit

Field-by-field justification

Mapped every form field to a business requirement. Challenged each one: is this strictly necessary? Removed 30% of fields from the baseline.

2

Structure

Progressive disclosure

Collapsed 4 steps into one scroll. Sections reveal contextually, keeping cognitive load minimal at each moment.

3

Detail

Inline validation, not post-submit

Errors surfaced field-by-field as users typed — never on submit. Correct fields stayed green. Progress was always preserved.

4

Trust

Contextual security signals

Lock icon and SSL indicator appear only when the payment section activates — not cluttering earlier steps.

01 — Problem

Abruzzo is Italy's best-kept secret. That's both its charm and its problem.

Abruzzo has mountains, coast, medieval villages, food culture, and national parks — all within a few hours of Rome. But its digital tourism infrastructure hadn't caught up. Activities were fragmented across dozens of local sites, WhatsApp groups, and word-of-mouth.

The brief: one app that connects both residents and visitors to the full range of experiences the region offers — without overwhelming either audience with content meant for the other.

02 — Research & Insights

Two audiences. Completely different mental models.

The most important early finding: residents and visitors approach discovery entirely differently. Visitors want to be guided. Residents want to be surprised. Designing for one risked alienating the other.

🗺️
Visitors need curation
"Tell me what's worth seeing." First-time visitors were overwhelmed by choice without editorial guidance. They needed a trusted voice, not a database.
🏡
Residents want discovery
"I've lived here 30 years and I didn't know that existed." Locals needed serendipitous discovery, not a tourist itinerary.
📅
Seasonality is invisible
Events and trails change dramatically by season. The app had to surface what's actually available right now — not a static catalogue.
📡
Offline reality
Mountain areas have limited signal. Any saved experience needed to work offline — including maps and booking confirmations.
03 — Process

One app. Two entry points. Shared core.

1

Strategy

Dual persona onboarding

Designed a single onboarding question — "Are you visiting or local?" — that branches the initial experience while sharing the same content architecture underneath.

2

Architecture

Category taxonomy

Mapped 30+ experience types to 6 top-level categories through card sorting with 12 participants. Zero overlap, natural groupings.

3

Design

Seasonal content system

Content cards surface availability dynamically — "open now", "seasonal", "bookable" — so the interface always reflects reality.

4

Resilience

Offline-first experience detail

Any saved experience renders fully offline — maps, directions, booking reference. Designed offline as a feature, not a fallback.

Discover → Experience detail → Offline map

One app. Two audiences. Zero compromises on either experience.

01 — Problem

Same template. Different company. No one could tell.

Dicarlobus is a regional bus operator in Abruzzo serving both local commuters and tourists. Their existing app was built on a generic public transport template shared with other operators — same layout, same components, barely differentiated branding.

The significant challenge: extracting a distinct brand identity from an application that shared a generic template with other public transportation services. Every design decision had to earn its place — not just look different, but mean something to the people who relied on this service daily.

02 — Research & Insights

Commuters and tourists. One app. Completely different urgency levels.

8 user interviews across two distinct groups: daily commuters who needed speed, and tourists who needed guidance and confidence. The existing app served neither — it was built for an abstract "transit user" who didn't exist.

⏱️
Commuters need speed
"I check it on the platform with 2 minutes to spare." Daily users needed their route in one tap — not buried under content meant for first-timers.
🧳
Tourists need reassurance
Visitors couldn't tell if the schedule was live or outdated. Trust signals — timestamps, real-time indicators — were completely absent.
🎨
Generic means invisible
Multiple users thought they'd used the same app for different bus companies. Zero brand recall eroded trust before the service was even evaluated.
♿
Accessibility failures
The existing interface failed WCAG AA on contrast, touch target size, and screen reader labelling — alienating a significant user base.
03 — Process

Identity first. Interface second.

1

Brand

Extracting identity from nothing

Built a colour system, type hierarchy, and iconography language from scratch — rooted in the amber and terracotta palette of Abruzzo's landscape. Starting from the one asset Dicarlobus owned: the name.

2

Structure

Context-aware homepage

Commuters see saved routes immediately. Tourists see curated routes and onboarding. Same architecture, different surface — adapts to returning vs. first-time users.

3

Clarity

Schedules as the product

Redesigned the schedule view as the primary product surface. Realtime status, departure countdown, and platform info scannable in under 2 seconds.

4

Accessibility

WCAG AA from the token level

Rebuilt colour system with contrast ratios baked into design tokens. Every touch target audited against 44×44px minimum. Screen reader labels reviewed on every element.

Context-aware home → Real-time schedule → Digital ticket

A generic template became a recognisable brand. Built from the ground up.
